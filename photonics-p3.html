Project Challenge

Commercial micro-manipulators for photonic chip work cost over £1,000, which puts high-precision positioning out of reach for teaching labs and small research groups. We wanted to see if we could build something with comparable performance using 3D printing and open-source hardware.

Design Goals

Get sub-micron positioning precision, maintain thermal stability over 24+ hours, integrate with microscope optics for real-time monitoring, and keep manufacturing cost under £100 using standard 3D printing.

Approach

OpenFlexure Delta Stage

Flexure-based design eliminates backlash and friction - key for good repeatability

Built-in Optics

Integrated microscope support for real-time alignment monitoring and characterization

MATLAB Analysis

Custom image processing to track motion and validate precision measurements

Thermal Management

External PCB mounting to minimize thermal drift from motor heating

Design Selection

We evaluated several open-source designs before picking the Delta Stage. The Block Stage had similar resolution, but the Delta Stage's integrated microscope optics made characterization much easier and gave better access for photonic coupling experiments.

Key Modifications

Thermal Fix

Moved control electronics outside the PLA frame to reduce thermal drift. Motor heating during operation was the biggest source of positioning error - external mounting cut this by over 80%.

Better Lighting

Replaced the fragile dovetail LED clip with a flexible arm design. Much more reliable and gives adjustable illumination angles for better chip visibility during alignment.

Performance Testing

Precision Results

  • X-axis steps: 51 nm (vs 58 nm theoretical)
  • Y-axis steps: 58 nm (vs 58 nm theoretical)
  • Z-axis steps: 35 nm (vs 58 nm theoretical)
  • Repeatability: ±2 µm across multiple cycles

Stability Testing

  • 24hr drift (unpowered): <2 µm total
  • 24hr drift (powered): 12-20 µm from thermal effects
  • Thermal behavior: Y-axis more sensitive than X-axis
  • Practical use: Good for multi-hour experiments

Real Performance Test

Achieved comparable coupling efficiency (-20 dBm) to the commercial NanoMax system (-18 dBm), with better long-term stability over 24-hour tests. Shows this approach actually works for real photonics applications.

Testing Methods

Developed testing protocols using USAF-1951 resolution targets and custom MATLAB image processing. Tracking calibration features let us measure step sizes and drift characteristics precisely across different time scales.

Motion Analysis

MATLAB algorithms analyzing microscope images to measure positioning accuracy and repeatability

Thermal Studies

24-hour drift measurements under different thermal conditions to validate stability

Coupling Tests

Statistics

Multiple measurement cycles to get confidence intervals and repeatability data

Impact

This project showed that open-source hardware can match expensive commercial equipment when properly designed and characterized. The dramatic cost reduction (£75 vs £1,000+) makes precision positioning accessible for education and budget-constrained research.

My contributions included MATLAB development for motion analysis, experimental design for thermal characterization, and systematic validation of positioning performance. The project taught me how important thermal effects are in precision mechanical systems and how to develop strategies to deal with them.

Broader Applications

The techniques extend beyond photonics to any application needing low-cost precision positioning: microscopy, materials testing, biological sample work, and educational lab setups. The open-source approach lets the broader research community customize and improve the design.

Future Improvements

Identified upgrades include active thermal compensation, inverted viewing for better optical access, and higher-resolution motors for even better precision. The modular design supports continuous development and optimization.
