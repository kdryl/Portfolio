Project Challenge

Design a compact, high-efficiency motor for a luxury EV that needs both high starting torque for acceleration and efficiency at highway speeds. The motor had to fit within tight packaging constraints while delivering professional-grade performance.

Design Goals

Hit 300 Nm torque at 3,000 RPM with field-oriented control, maintain 90%+ efficiency across the operating range, and scale up to 18,000 RPM through field weakening. Validate everything with finite element analysis.

Design Approach

Starting Point

Used Excel-based design tools following standard AC machine design principles for initial sizing

Materials

Selected NO20 silicon steel laminations to minimize iron losses and get good magnetic properties

Configuration

Went with 4-pole design and 5 slots per pole per phase to reduce harmonics

Validation

Used FEMM finite element simulations to check the analytical predictions

Design Process

Started with established electromagnetic machine design principles, working from power and torque specs through magnetic circuit analysis and thermal considerations. Each design choice was checked with both calculations and finite element modeling.

Stator Cross-Section Design

CAD model showing tooth geometry, slot layout, and magnetic flux paths

Key Design Decisions

Magnetic Circuit

  • 4-pole design for better slot fill
  • 5 slots per pole per phase to minimize harmonics
  • Optimized tooth tips to reduce airgap reluctance
  • 1.5 T airgap flux for maximum power density

Winding Design

  • Double-layer distributed winding
  • 25 turns per phase with 5 coils in series
  • 4 parallel paths for current handling
  • SWG19 multi-strand wire for thermal management

Manufacturing Reality

The design had to work in the real world, so I included practical constraints like slot liners, insulation requirements, and winding access. Phase separation and end-winding geometry were optimized for both electrical performance and manufacturability.

Performance Results

Electrical Parameters

Magnetizing inductance: 3.46 mH, Leakage inductance: 0.173 mH, Phase resistance: 25 mΩ at 160°C

Operating Range

Peak torque: 300 Nm at 3,000 RPM, Corner speed: 7,500 RPM at 150 Nm, Max speed: 18,000 RPM

Loss Analysis

Iron loss calculations using NO20 material data with 1.5× build factor for manufacturing tolerances

Validation Success

FEMM simulations confirmed 1.5 T airgap flux density and 68 A peak magnetizing current. Magnetic energy calculations matched analytical models within 5% - close enough to validate the design approach.

FEMM Simulation Results

Finite element analysis showing flux density distribution and saturation regions

Control System Integration

Field-Oriented Control

Built a complete MATLAB/Simulink model implementing vector control. Demonstrated motor behavior under varying loads, field weakening operation, and transient response for EV drive cycles.

Thermal Design

Sized conductors for 160°C operation with proper thermal derating. Phase resistance calculations included temperature effects and verified current density for continuous operation.

Real-World Application

The design methods and results translate directly to commercial EV motor development. The approach balances electromagnetic performance, thermal management, and manufacturing constraints in a way that scales to different power ratings and vehicle applications.

What I Learned

This project taught me how electromagnetic machine design works from theory through practical implementation. The combination of analytical design methods with finite element validation gave me a solid framework for motor development in the EV industry.

Key technical achievements included integrating analytical and FEA methods, implementing field-oriented control, and understanding the trade-offs between electromagnetic performance, thermal limits, and manufacturing reality in high-performance motor design.
